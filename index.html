Trust is not a vibe. It is something you measure.

Jordan Chambers is a Master of Software Development graduate working on retrieval-augmented generation systems for scientific computing at the University of Utah.

The throughline in Jordan's work is trust: not as a philosophical abstraction, but as an engineering property that has to be tested, instrumented, and made visible.

Her path into software began in psychology, where she was drawn to the question of whether decision-making systems can actually be trusted. Psychology offered the question, but not always the ground truth. Code gave her a harder surface to test against.

The move was less a pivot than a change in tools. Her MSD capstone applied machine learning to anomaly detection in behavioral activity logs, using supervised models, forecast models, rules, and anomaly detection to ask a practical version of the same question: when a system flags risk, how do we know whether that signal deserves attention?

Jordan's current research carries that standard into agentic AI and LLM-based systems. The present proving ground is RAG for scientific codebases, where retrieval quality can be evaluated against source code, mathematical context, and executable behavior. If the system cannot show its work, measure its limits, and survive evaluation, she does not treat it as trustworthy.

Current focus

Jordan is especially interested in teams building agentic systems, retrieval-augmented generation, AI evaluation, and scientific computing tools where correctness matters more than polish. Her work sits between research and engineering: define the failure mode, build the harness, evaluate the system, then improve the parts that actually moved the metric.

Relevant areas: RAG evaluation, code retrieval, scientific software, anomaly detection, behavioral data, agentic AI, measurement-first engineering.
